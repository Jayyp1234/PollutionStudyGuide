Module I · The framing

Introduction to the Environmental Problem

The keywords, defined

  • Environment — everything that surrounds living organisms. It comprises the atmosphere (air), hydrosphere (water bodies), lithosphere (solid earth, soil & rock) and the biosphere — the thin shell where the other three meet and which makes life on earth possible.
  • Pollution — to pollute is to defile, corrupt, contaminate. Formally: an undesirable change in the physical, chemical, or biological characteristics of air, water, or soil that may harmfully affect life or create a potential health hazard for any living organism.
  • Engineering — the profession that uses scientific principles to either meet man's need or solve man's problem. Environmental engineering applies these principles to both: meet need without creating environmental harm, and solve the problems already created.

How did the environmental problem start?

1. Human population growth

Before the Industrial Revolution, world population grew slowly and almost linearly. After the eighteenth century the growth rate became exponential. More people = more demand for food, energy, materials, transport, and waste sinks.

2. Industrialization

The Industrial Revolution began the cycle of industrial growth that has produced wonders — and consequences. New chemicals are developed every day; many find their way into the environment via accidental leakage, manufacture, transport, or usage.

The course outline tracks five "Industrial Revolutions" — the most recent (5.0) about integrating AI more effectively into society.

Three notorious case studies

  • The Bhopal Tragedy (1984, India) — methyl isocyanate gas leak from a Union Carbide pesticide plant, killing thousands and injuring hundreds of thousands.
  • Erin Brockovich (Hinkley, California) — hexavalent chromium contamination of groundwater by Pacific Gas & Electric, dramatised in the 2000 film.
  • Love Canal (Niagara Falls, NY, 1978) — homes and a school built on a buried toxic waste dump; the resulting health crisis triggered Superfund legislation.

All three illustrate how routine industrial activity can produce slow-moving, devastating environmental disasters when control fails.

Ecosystems: the units of the biosphere

The biosphere is built from many smaller ecosystems. Each ecosystem has two component types:

  • Abiotic components — non-living: phosphorus, nitrogen, water, sunlight, soil minerals.
  • Biotic components — living, which split further into:
    • Producers — self-nourishing (e.g. plants).
    • Consumers — eat producers to sustain life (animals, fish, insects, birds, mammals).
    • Decomposers — bacteria and fungi breaking down dead organic matter back into chemical components for producers.

Until recently, human changes to ecosystems were small perturbations on a large stable system, absorbed by feedback loops (the homeostatic mechanisms that keep ecosystems stable). Today, perturbations are large enough to permanently upset natural cycles — and that is when we have pollution.

Two-way impact

Impact of man on the environment → Exploitation

Impact of environment on man → Pollution

Once we cross the homeostatic threshold and natural cycles are disrupted, the environment begins to impact us back — and that impact is what we call pollution.

Air Pollution & its Effects

What is air pollution?

Air pollution
An imbalance in the ratio of gases normally present in air — caused by the addition of substances or energy in concentrations & durations that may harm life or impair the environment.

"The earth is our mother — whatever befalls the earth befalls the sons and daughters of the earth."

Composition of normal air (sea level, 15 °C, 1 atm)

Gas% by volume
Nitrogen — N278.084 %
Oxygen — O220.948 %
Argon — Ar0.934 %
Carbon dioxide — CO20.0314 %
Neon — Ne0.001818 %
Methane — CH40.0002 %
Helium / Krypton / Hydrogen / Xenon≤ 0.0005 % each
Ozone, NO2, I2, CO, NH3trace

Air pollution is, fundamentally, the disturbance of these proportions — usually by adding too much of a trace gas, or introducing one not on the list at all.

Causes — natural

  • Forest fires
  • Ash & sulphur gases from volcanoes
  • Dust storms (esp. harmattan in West Africa)
  • Gases & odours from natural decomposition
  • Gases from swamps
  • Esters & terpenes from vegetation
  • Ozone from lightning
  • Natural radioactivity

Causes — anthropogenic (man-made)

  • Population explosion
  • Deforestation
  • Urbanisation
  • Industrialisation

Major industrial sources: thermal power stations and automobiles. In most major cities, vehicular exhaust accounts for:

  • 70 % of CO
  • 50 % of HCs
  • 30–40 % of nitrogen oxides
  • 30 % of particulate matter

Classification of pollution sources

Stationary (point) sources

  • Point — a fixed point such as a smokestack or storage tank, emitting from a single discharge location.
  • Area — many small stationary sources that together affect regional air quality. Example: a community of homes using kerosene stoves — each contributes a tiny amount, but the aggregate matters.

Mobile sources

  • Line — highway vehicles along a road.
  • Area — aircraft, port vessels, and others spread over a region.

Primary pollutants — released directly

  • Carbon compounds — CO2, CO
  • Sulphur compounds — SO2, H2S
  • Nitrogen oxides — NO, NO2, HNO3
  • Fluorocarbons — insecticides, CFCs
  • Ozone — O3 (also a secondary pollutant in tropospheric smog)
  • Metals — lead, nickel, arsenic, cadmium
  • Particulate matter — fly ash, dust, grit, soot (solid, liquid droplets, or gases)
  • Toxicants — complex chemical substances released during manufacture

Secondary pollutants — POPs (Photochemical Oxidation Products)

Formed by photochemical reactions between NOx, hydrocarbons, and O3 in the atmosphere in the presence of sunlight. POPs are often more toxic than the primary pollutants that cause them.

Smog formation (simplified)
NOx + HCs + O3 + sunlight → POPs

Principal POPs:

  • PAN — peroxyacetyl nitrate (CH3C(O)OONO2)
  • Peroxybenzoyl nitrate
  • Ozone at ground level
  • Aldehydes & olefins

Photochemical smog becomes problematic during atmospheric inversions, when pollutants can't disperse vertically.

Effects of air pollution — overview

Four broad categories:

  1. Atmospheric characteristics — visibility reduction (light scattering), fog formation around dust nuclei, reduced solar radiation at ground level, altered temperature and wind distribution.
  2. Vegetation — leaf damage, reduced growth, reduced crop yield (with measurable concentration thresholds and exposure times).
  3. Human health — directly (inhalation, ingestion) and indirectly (skin contact). The most damaging effects occur in the respiratory system.
  4. Materials — corrosion of metals, discolouration of buildings/paints, weakening of leather/rubber/paper, fading of fabrics.

"Effects are not meaningful unless exposure time and threshold concentration are given." — i.e. dose & duration, not just presence.

Vegetation thresholds — dose-response data

PollutantSymptomThreshold (ppm / μg m-3)Sustained exposure

Units of measurement: ppm ↔ μg/m³

Concentrations of gaseous pollutants can be reported in either parts-per-million by volume or in mass per unit volume. The conversion at 25 °C, 1 atm is:

Standard conversion
1 ppm = 40.9 × MW μg/m3

Derivation:

PV = nRT → n/V = P/RT = 101325 / (8314 × 298) = 40.90 mol/m3 per m3 of pure pollutant
1 ppm = 1 m3 pollutant per 106 m3 air = 40.9 × MW · 10-6 g/m3 = 40.9 × MW μg/m3

Tip: the side-tool calculator (Σ button) does this for you live — or open it manually with C.

The respiratory system — defenses against particulates

Air enters the trachea, which divides into bronchi (left & right) leading to the lungs. The bronchi divide and re-divide, ending in millions of tiny air sacs (alveoli) where O2/CO2 exchange happens. Particulates are filtered out at three lines of defense:

  • Nostril hairs — 1st line: 95 % of particles with diameter Dp > 5 μm are blown out or swallowed.
  • Cilia in trachea / bronchus — 2nd line: Dp < 5 μm get coughed out.
  • Brownian motion — Dp < 0.01 μm reach the lungs but are easily exhaled.

Problematic range: 0.01 μm < Dp < 0.1 μm — these get deposited in the alveoli where they can:

  • Cause extra resistance to O2/CO2 diffusion
  • Chemically react with lung tissue
  • Be absorbed into the bloodstream through the thin alveolar membrane

Specific particulates — health effects & sources

ParticulateEffectSource
LeadAnemia, high BP, brain & kidney damage, neurological disorders, cancer, lowered IQMetal refineries, lead smelters, battery manufacturers, iron & steel producers

Standards report particulate matter by mass (μg/m³), but inert particles often carry toxic substances on their surfaces. The substances doing the harm are not always the particles themselves.

WHO PM2.5 guideline (2021 update)

Recommended annual mean
PM2.5 ≤ 5 μg/m3 (down from 10 μg/m3)

According to the AQLI (2022), 97.3 % of the world's population is exposed to PM2.5 levels above WHO guidelines. This takes ~2.2 years off global average life expectancy. In South Asia residents may lose up to 5 years; 37 of the world's 40 most polluted cities are there.

Fine particles travel hundreds of kilometres — ~30 % of pollution in India's Punjab originates in Pakistan; ~30 % in Bangladesh's largest cities is traced back to India.

Gaseous effects — solubility decides where damage occurs

The gas defense system is the body's weakest: gases dissolve into the moist mucus that coats the respiratory tract. Where damage happens depends on solubility:

Solubility order (low → high)
CO < O2 < CO2 < SO2 < NH3
  • Soluble gases (SO2, NH3) — absorbed in the upper respiratory tract, causing bronchial constriction. The body produces more mucus to fend off the irritant — vicious cycle.
  • Less soluble gases (CO, NOx) — pass deeper, into the alveoli, where they compete with O2 for binding sites in haemoglobin.

Hb has 210× more affinity for CO than for O2. CO-haemoglobin (COHb) also prevents release of O2 at tissues.

Pollution Episodes — when atmosphere fails to disperse

Ventilation depends on two mechanisms:

  • Horizontal wind flow — cessation gives calm.
  • Vertical convection — suppression gives atmospheric inversion.
CALM + INVERSION sustained > 36 hrs = STAGNATION
POLLUTION BUILDUP + STAGNATION = EPISODE

The 3-way catalytic converter

A stainless-steel cylinder containing a ceramic honeycomb filler coated with catalysing metals (platinum, rhodium, palladium). Mounted in the exhaust line of petrol vehicles.

Reduces:

  • CO emissions by 80 %
  • HC emissions by 90 %
  • NOx reduced via redox over Rh

Requires lead-free fuel — lead poisons the catalyst, irreversibly destroying its activity. This is why leaded petrol was phased out worldwide.

Global effects — local actions, planetary consequences

Three classic global air-pollution problems where one country's emission is another country's damage:

  1. Acid rain
  2. Global warming
  3. Ozone layer depletion

1. Acid rain

Causes: combustion of fuel, transportation, industrial SO2 and NOx emissions (which form H2SO4 and HNO3 with atmospheric water).

Effects:

  • Lowers pH of water bodies — toxic to aquatic life; increases dissolved-metal toxicity
  • Increases soil acidity → decreased nutrient availability and release of toxic metals
  • Disintegrates buildings, statues, monuments

Cross-border injustice: Scandinavian countries suffer most from European emissions; Canada from US emissions. The polluter and the affected country are often different.

2. Global warming — the greenhouse gases

Causes: emissions of greenhouse gases (GHGs) and removal of GHG sinks (deforestation).

GHGGWP (CO2-equivalent over 100 yrs)
Carbon dioxide (CO2)1 (reference)
Methane (CH4)21
Nitrous oxide (N2O)310
Hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs)140 – 11,000
Perfluorocarbons (PFCs)6,500 – 9,200
Sulphur hexafluoride (SF6)23,900

Effects: extreme weather (droughts, floods); rising sea level (~75 % of environmental water is locked in icecaps & glaciers); shifts in agricultural climate zones; new disease vectors as pathogens spread.

A counter-question often raised: CO2 is 0.0314 % on Earth, 95.3 % on Mars (cold), 96.5 % on Venus (hot). What explains the difference? Atmospheric density: Earth ≈ 1.22 kg/m³, Mars ≈ 0.02 kg/m³, Venus ≈ 65 kg/m³ — Mars's thin atmosphere can't retain heat despite high CO2 %.

3. Ozone layer depletion — the chemistry

The atmosphere has 4 main layers (with the exosphere highest):

  1. Troposphere — where weather happens; people, plants, animals live here
  2. Stratosphere — contains the ozone layer that blocks UV radiation
  3. Mesosphere — coldest at top, warmest at bottom
  4. Thermosphere — temperature begins rising again; merges into the exosphere above

Ozone formation in the stratosphere

hν + O2 → 2 O· (oxygen radicals)
O2 + O· → O3 (ozone)
O3 + O· → 2 O2 (continuous regeneration)

Ozone destruction by CFCs

CFCl3 + hν → CFCl2 + Cl·
Cl· + O3 → ClO + O2
ClO + O· → Cl· + O2 ← chain repeats!

A single chlorine atom in the stratosphere can destroy up to 100,000 ozone molecules.

Sources of ozone-depleting CFCs

  • Refrigeration / air-conditioning coolants
  • Blowing agents in foam manufacture
  • Aerosol propellants (perfumes, sprays)
  • Fire extinguishers
  • Solvents for electronic-component cleaning, dry cleaning

Effects of stratospheric ozone loss

  • Destroys phytoplankton — collapses the marine food chain at the base
  • Reduces crop yield
  • Eye cataracts
  • Skin cancer
  • Suppression of immune system → increased disease risk

CFCs were replaced by HFCs (ODP 0.005–0.2). But HFCs have GWP 140–11,000 — so we traded ozone depletion for a global-warming problem. The irony: HFC refrigeration cools rooms while the leaks warm the planet, requiring more cooling, requiring more leaks…

Energy & the Environment

The energy story in one number

Energy is the capacity to do work. Per-capita energy use has risen from about 8 MJ/person/day at the dawn of agriculture to ~1,000 MJ/person/day today — a 125× increase. Most of that growth happened in the last 200 years and is fed almost entirely by fossil fuels.

Major non-renewable energy sources

1. Fossil fuels (coal, crude oil, natural gas)

Non-renewable — rate of extraction >>> rate of formation. Combustion in industry or transportation is the chief source of NOx, CO2, SOx, and unburnt hydrocarbons.

  • Coal is the dirtiest fossil fuel. It supplies ~41 % of the world's electricity.
  • Top users: China #1, India #2, USA #3.
  • The US has only 4.23 % of world population and ~3 % of world fossil reserves, but consumes ~25 % of world fossil fuel — herein lies the politics. Many developed countries (USA, UK) want to reduce reliance on imports from politically unstable regions.

Current fossil-fuel use is widely held to be unsustainable on three axes — Environmentally, Economically, and Socially.

The Triple Bottom Line — the 3 Ps of sustainability

Sustainable development requires
People · Planet · Profit

Decisions that ignore any one P aren't truly sustainable.

Fracking — hydraulic fracturing

Fracking extracts oil and gas trapped in shale and other rock formations by pumping water + sand + chemicals down a well at high pressure. The high pressure fractures the rock, releasing gas/oil that flows back up.

Made the US "energy independent" / "dominant" — but with side effects:

  • May trigger earthquakes (high pressures + on-site wastewater storage)
  • Groundwater contamination concerns (chemicals + methane migration)
  • Methane leaks from extraction add to GHG burden

Nuclear (Uranium-235)

Non-renewable, but vast deposits exist (e.g. Australia). 1 kg of uranium oxide produces heat equivalent to ~16 metric tonnes (16,000 kg) of coal — phenomenal energy density.

Concerns

  • Enrichment produces toxic waste
  • Spent rods (highly radioactive) currently stored in deep caves and mines
  • Nuclear plants are delicate (unstable) — major accidents:
    • 1979 · Three Mile Island, Pennsylvania, USA
    • 1986 · Chernobyl, Ukraine (then USSR)
    • 2011 · Fukushima Daiichi, Japan
  • Nuclear weapons proliferation, especially in politically unstable regions

Sustainable option 1 — Energy efficiency

Meeting demand with dwindling supply — getting more from less.

  • Conservation — LED & fluorescent bulbs replace incandescents; switch off lights/AC when leaving; walk / bike / bus / carpool; natural lighting & ventilation; solar panels / inverters instead of generators; use less water.
  • Increased equipment efficiency — better designs that yield more useful output per unit input.
  • Cogeneration (CHP) — capture and use waste heat instead of releasing it to the atmosphere.

Sustainable option 2 — Alternative (renewable) sources

  • Biofuel — fuel from biomass
  • Biomass — organic matter directly
  • Geothermal — hot rock formations to produce heat
  • Hydroelectric — energy in falling water
  • Solar — electromagnetic radiation from the sun
  • Tidal — rise & fall of sea level
  • Wave — ocean surface waves
  • Wind — kinetic energy of moving air

The reality check

Despite all alternatives, fossil fuels still provide ~70 % of global electrical power and ~95 % of total energy demand (heating, transport, electricity). They will likely remain dominant for some decades. The question is not whether we use them — it's how quickly we can shift, and how well we control emissions while doing so.

Energy & the environment — closing thought

Energy production and consumption are tightly coupled to environmental outcomes. Burning fossil fuels releases GHGs that drive global warming and climate change; it also creates air and water pollution that affects human health and ecosystems.

Renewables offer cleaner alternatives but they are not zero-impact: hydro disrupts river ecosystems and displaces communities; solar farms occupy land; wind turbines kill birds and bats. The challenge is balance — energy needs vs environmental protection, ramping up alternatives faster than fossil dependence reduces them.

Simple Interest
I = P · i · n
P = principal, i = rate per period, n = periods.
SI Future Value
FV = P (1 + in)
Or simply FV = P + I.
Compound Interest FV
FV = P (1 + i)ⁿ
Used in nearly all ECN analyses.
Compound Interest PV
PV = FV / (1 + i)ⁿ
Discounting future to present.
Annuity (Capital Recovery)
A = P i (1+i)ⁿ / [(1+i)ⁿ − 1]
Equal annual payment from a present sum.
Real Interest Rate
1 + i_nom = (1 + i_real)(1 + F)
F = inflation rate.
Straight-Line Depreciation
D = (P − S) / n
Equal depreciation each year.
Declining Balance
D_t = Rate × Book value
Front-loaded; used when value drops fast.
Sum of Year Digits
D_t = (P−S)·(n−t+1) / [n(n+1)/2]
